CX isn't broken. Your feedback loops are.

Most organizations don't have a CX problem: they have a feedback loop problem. The gap between what customers say and what companies actually do is where loyalty quietly dies.

Contents

  • Why feedback dies in the middle
  • Issues you can't fix yet
  • Transparency and operational courage

If there's one uncomfortable truth I've learned after digging through customer verbatims, running VoC dashboards, and building personas and journey maps, it's this: most organizations don't have a CX problem: they have a feedback loop problem.

And that gap, between what customers say and what organizations actually do, is the real black hole where loyalty, trust, and growth quietly erode.

1. Why feedback dies in the middle

95% of companies ask for feedback, but only 10% act on it, and 5% tell their customers what they've done.

Every organization collects feedback. Fewer know how to interpret it well. And a much smaller group knows how to act on it consistently, transparently, and across functions.

Here's what usually happens: a customer leaves a negative review. It gets tagged. It gets queued. The queue grows, spillover builds, the team gets overwhelmed. Eventually a root cause is spotted, but the fix needs another team. That team has other priorities. And so the issue lives forever as "to be improved next quarter."

Closing the loop isn't just "responding to complaints." It's a discipline, a system, a promise to customers, and honestly, a bit of a cultural shift. A strong closing-the-loop program does three things:

  • Acknowledges: customers know you heard them.
  • Explains: they understand the why behind the issue.
  • Improves: you make real changes, or explain why you can't yet.

That last part is where most organizations stumble.

2. What about issues you can't fix yet?

Sometimes you can't close the loop immediately: the fix is too big, too costly, too political, or not yet technically feasible. But "we can't fix it now" is not a license to stay silent. It's a moment to practice transparent CX:

  • Acknowledge the signal: "We saw what you shared."
  • Explain honestly, without hiding behind jargon: "This requires changes to X, which we're actively scoping."
  • Offer what you can today: workarounds, human support, alternatives.
  • Set expectations: "Here's when you'll hear from us, even if the update is that nothing has changed yet."
  • Document the signal volume so leaders feel the pressure build over time.

Customers don't demand perfection. They demand respect: being kept in the loop.

3. The future of CX is transparency plus operational courage

The real currency of CX now is speed: speed of learning, speed of fixing, speed of communicating. A closing-the-loop culture is how organizations shrink the distance between signal, sense-making, and action.
